Work deeper, not faster. That is the whole reason a borrowed AI workflow rarely sticks.

I watch people copy a setup they saw online, run it once, and quietly drop it a week later. It is not a discipline problem. The workflow was built for someone else's work, and it shows.

Three reasons a copied workflow fails:

The task is not yours. Their bottleneck is not your bottleneck. A tool tuned for their week wastes motion in yours.

## Angle 2: The loop that builds it

### Instagram Post

You do not need a course to build your own AI workflow. You need a loop.

Here is the one I use.

Pick a task you repeat. Boring and weekly is perfect.

Write down every step the way you already do it by hand. All of it.

Hand the AI one step. Just one. The most annoying one.

Check its work. You keep the last word, always.

Then repeat. Once that step is solid, hand it one more.

That is it. You are not building a robot. You are teaching a helper one move at a time, and staying in charge of the whole thing.

Most people try to hand over everything on day one and it falls apart. Go one step at a time and it holds.

Save this and try it on one task this week.

### LinkedIn Post

The way you get real value out of AI is a loop you can run on any task, no course required.

Most people approach AI backward. They try to automate a whole job at once, it breaks in three places, and they decide the tool is not for them. The job was too big for a first pass. Shrink it.

Here is the loop I teach beginners:

1. Task. Pick one thing you repeat. The more routine, the better. Routine work has clear steps, which makes it easy to hand off.

2. Steps. Write down how you do it now, by hand, in order. This is the part people skip, and it is the part that matters most. You cannot delegate a process you have never named.

3. Hand off. Give the AI exactly one step. The most tedious one. Not the whole task. One move.

4. Check. Read what it produced. You keep the last word. This step never leaves you, no matter how good the tool gets.

5. Repeat. Once that step is reliable, add one more. The workflow grows by one link at a time.

Notice what this loop protects. You always know what the AI is doing, because you handed it each piece yourself. And the judgment stays with you, because checking is built into every pass.

A workflow built this way is slow to start and hard to break. That is the trade you want.

The first AI workflow I built was not fancy. It was gig follow-ups.

After a show I would tell myself I would email the venue tomorrow. Thank them, ask about the next date. Half the time tomorrow never came. Good rooms went cold because I was tired and behind.

So I built a tiny workflow. Pick the venue. Map how I would normally write the note. Let the AI draft the first version off my rough notes. Then I read it, make it sound like me, and send it.

Four steps. Fifteen minutes a week instead of never.

The AI does not send anything. It just gets me past the blank page. I still write the real closing line and I still hit send.

That one small workflow got me more repeat bookings than any big idea I ever tried.
