## Angle 1: Speed is not the problem

### Instagram Post

Everyone sells AI as a way to go faster. For a working creative, faster is rarely the actual problem.

I was never short on speed. I was short on hours where I could think clearly. More output at the same shallow depth does not help. It just gives me more to clean up later.

The real win is not doing the same work quicker. It is doing the work deeper. One follow-up that actually lands. One idea taken all the way instead of five left half-done.

AI is fast. That part is easy. The hard part, the part worth your time, is the judgment about what is good. That still belongs to you.

Speed feels like progress. Depth is progress.

### LinkedIn Post

Work deeper, not faster. That is the wedge I keep coming back to with AI, and it runs against almost every pitch you hear.

The default promise is speed. Do the same work in half the time. For a working creative that promise misses the real bottleneck. My constraint was never how fast I could crank out an email or a caption. It was how much good judgment I could bring to the things that mattered before I ran out of hours in the day.

When you point AI only at speed, you get more output at the same shallow depth. More drafts, more posts, more replies, none of them better. And now you have more to review, which is the part that was scarce to begin with.

Here is the shift that actually helped me. I stopped asking AI to make me faster and started asking it to clear the low-value work off my plate so I had room to go deep on the high-value work. The follow-up that wins a booking. The idea taken all the way through. The message that sounds like a person, not a template.

The tool handles the volume. You handle the depth. That trade only works if you stay in the loop and keep the judgment for yourself, because depth is the thing a model cannot supply for you.

### LinkedIn Post

Work deeper, not faster starts with a simple question most people skip. Not what can AI do. Which task do I keep redoing every week that I dread?

I teach beginners a short audit for exactly this. Four steps, about ten minutes, and it turns a vague sense that AI could help into one concrete place to start.

1. List. Write down the tasks you repeat every week. Not the creative centerpiece, the recurring admin around it. Booking replies, caption first drafts, thank-you notes, scheduling.

2. Rank by dread. Not by how long it takes. By how much you avoid it. The task you keep pushing to tomorrow is the one draining you, and it is where a first draft from AI buys back the most.

3. Draft, do not delegate. Point AI at that one task and let it produce a rough first pass. First pass, not final. The goal is a running start, not a finished product you paste without reading.

4. Keep the judgment. You edit it, you make it sound like you, you decide if it ships. The model handles the blank page. You handle whether it is any good.

The mistake I see is people trying to automate ten things at once and abandoning all of them. Pick one dreaded recurring task. Prove it works. Then add the next.

Run the audit this week and name your one task.

When people ask me to prove AI is useful for a working creative, I do not hand them a theory. I show them the one place I put it first, and exactly how I keep the judgment.
