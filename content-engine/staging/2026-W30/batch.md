## Angle 1: Why AI flattens your voice

### Instagram Post

You paste something into AI and it comes back sounding fine. That is the problem.

Fine is the trap. A model is trained on millions of writers, so it aims for the average of all of them. The middle. And the middle has no edges.

Your voice lives in the edges. The odd word you always reach for. The way you start a sentence too short. The joke you would make in the room.

AI sands all of that off. It picks the safe word over your word every time. Clean, correct, and completely nobody.

People do not follow you because you sound correct. They follow you because you sound like you.

So when AI hands you fine, do not ship it. That is the first draft, not the last.

Save this for the next time a draft comes back sounding a little too smooth.

### LinkedIn Post

Most people ask AI to write faster. The better question is what you lose when it does.

A language model is an averaging machine. It was trained on an enormous pile of human writing, and it predicts the most likely next word across all of it. Likely is the operative word. It reaches for what most people would say, not what you would say.

That is why AI copy so often reads as competent and forgettable at the same time. Nothing is wrong with it. Nothing is yours either.

Here is where the flattening happens.

It picks safe words. Your actual vocabulary has quirks. The model trades them for the common choice.

It smooths rhythm. You might write a three word sentence on purpose. The model evens everything into the same medium length.

It removes the specific. Your best lines come from things only you have seen. A model has not lived your work, so it fills the gap with the generic.

None of this makes AI useless. It makes it a starting point, not a finished voice. The draft is cheap now. The voice is the part that still has to come from you.

If your last ten posts could have been written by anyone, that is the real cost of letting the tool talk for you.

Look at your recent AI drafts and ask one thing. Does this sound like me, or does it just sound correct.

## Angle 2: The voice keeper pass

### Instagram Post

You do not have to choose between using AI and sounding like yourself. You just need a pass at the end.

Here is mine. Four steps, about two minutes.

Feed. Before AI writes, give it your own words first. A voice note, a messy paragraph, an old post you liked. It can only echo what you feed it.

Cut. Read the draft and delete the fancy lines. The ones that sound impressive and say nothing. Those are never yours.

Add. Put in one thing only you know. A specific from your actual week. That single detail does more than a whole paragraph of polish.

Read. Say it out loud. If you would never talk like that in a room, rewrite it.

The tool does the bones. You do the voice. That is the whole idea behind Human-Led AI.

### LinkedIn Post

The goal with AI is not to write faster. It is to write deeper without losing whose writing it is. Speed is easy. Keeping your voice takes a deliberate pass.

Here is the one I teach. Four steps, and it runs in about two minutes on anything a model hands you.

1. Feed. Garbage in, generic out. Before you ask AI to draft, give it your raw material first. A voice memo you talked through, a rough paragraph in your own words, two or three past posts you were proud of. The model cannot invent your voice. It can only reflect what you put in front of it.

2. Cut. Read the draft with a red pen for anything that sounds like a brochure. The lines that feel impressive and mean nothing. That is the model reaching for polish. Delete them without mercy. What is left is closer to you already.

3. Add. Put in one thing only you could know. A specific from your actual week, a number from your own work, a small moment from the room. One real detail beats a paragraph of clean filler.

4. Read. Say the whole thing out loud. Your ear catches what your eye skips. If a line is not how you would say it to a colleague, rewrite it in plain speech.

The model does the structure. You keep the voice, because the voice is the part that carries your name.

Run this pass on your next AI draft and watch how much of the machine you end up cutting.

## Angle 3: Make it sound like you

**Graphic:** IG `make-it-sound-like-you-1x1.png` (1080×1080) · LI/FB `make-it-sound-like-you-4x5.png` (1080×1350) · data `make-it-sound-like-you.json`

### Instagram Post

Watch me take a stiff AI draft and make it sound like a person.

The draft said something like: I am thrilled to share some exciting updates about my upcoming workshop. Nobody talks like that. Not even the person who wrote it.

So I run four quick moves.

Draft. I let AI do the bones. Structure, order, the boring scaffolding. Fine.

Break. I chop the long sentences. Short lines sound like breathing. Long ones sound like a memo.

Swap. I trade every stiff word for the one I would actually say. Thrilled becomes glad. Exciting updates becomes what I have been working on.

Sign. I add one line only I would write. A small aside, a real detail. The fingerprint.

Same information. Completely different human on the page.

### LinkedIn Post

Everyone can spot AI writing now. The tell is not bad grammar. It is that it sounds like no one. Here is how I take a generic draft and put a person back into it, using a real example.

I asked a model to draft a post about a workshop. It gave me the usual: I am thrilled to share some exciting updates about an upcoming session designed to help you get more out of your tools. Clean. Lifeless.

Four moves fix it.

Draft. I let AI do the bones. The order, the structure, the parts that are just plumbing. That is genuinely where it saves time, and I do not fight it there.

Break. AI loves the long, even sentence. I chop them. A short line lands. A run-on drifts. Reading rhythm is half of what makes writing sound like a specific human.

Swap. I go word by word and trade the stiff choice for mine. Thrilled becomes glad. Commence becomes start. Exciting updates becomes what I have actually been building. Small swaps, big difference.

Sign. I add one line only I would write. An aside, a specific from my week, a bit of dry humor. That single line is the fingerprint that says a person made this, not a prompt.

The result carries the same information the model gave me, but now it sounds like me. That is the whole job. The machine drafts. The human signs.

Take one AI draft today and run those four moves on it before you post.
