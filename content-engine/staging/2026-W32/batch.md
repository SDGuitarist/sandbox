## Angle 2: The four questions that protect your likeness

**Graphic:** IG `the-4-cs-framework-1x1.png` (1080×1080) · LI/FB `the-4-cs-framework-4x5.png` (1080×1350) · data `the-4-cs-framework.json`

### Instagram Post

When someone wants to use your voice, face, or style with AI, run it through four questions. I call them the 4 C's.

Consent. Did you actually say yes to this exact use? A yes for one thing is not a yes for everything.

Control. Can you pull it back later? A use you cannot stop is not really yours anymore.

Compensation. Are you paid for it? If your likeness makes money, some of that is yours.

Credit. Is your name on it? People should know it is you, and know when it is not.

Miss one and the deal is lopsided, even if it feels friendly in the moment.

You do not need a lawyer to ask these. You need to ask them out loud before you agree, not after.

Save this and run your next request through all four.

### LinkedIn Post

People keep asking me how to protect their likeness from AI without hiring a lawyer for every conversation. Here is the plain-language check I use. Four questions. I call them the 4 C's.

Consent. Did you agree to this specific use? Consent is not a switch you flip once. Saying yes to a headshot for a website is not saying yes to that face in an ad two years later. Scope the yes to the exact use, and assume nothing carries over.

Control. Can you take it back? A license you cannot revoke is a decision you only get to make once. Real control means there is a way to say stop, and a date when the permission ends on its own if nobody renews it.

Compensation. If your likeness is doing work, are you paid for that work? This does not always mean cash up front. It means the value is shared on purpose, not quietly kept by whoever holds the file.

Credit. Is it clear that this is you, and clear when something is not? Credit protects your reputation in both directions. It attaches your name to what is real and keeps it off what is fake.

None of these require legal training to ask. They require asking before you agree, in words a normal person understands.

Run your next AI request through all four and watch where it falls short. That gap is the conversation you actually need to have.

## Angle 3: Putting the 4 C's on paper

**Graphic:** IG `put-it-in-writing-1x1.png` (1080×1080) · LI/FB `put-it-in-writing-4x5.png` (1080×1350) · data `put-it-in-writing.json`

### Instagram Post

A handshake yes does not protect your likeness. Writing it down does.

When you turn the 4 C's into an actual agreement, five things need to be on the page.

Scope. What can they use, and where. Name the platforms and the projects.

Term. For how long. Every permission needs an end date.

Kill switch. How you revoke it, and how fast they have to stop.

Pay. Flat fee or per use, spelled out, not implied.

Byline. How you are credited, so nobody has to guess it is you.

None of this has to be fancy. A short clear email that both people agree to beats a friendly nod every time.

Vague is how you get used. Specific is how you stay in charge.

Save this before your next yes.

### LinkedIn Post

The 4 C's are worth nothing if they live in your head. The work is turning consent, control, compensation, and credit into words on a page that both sides agree to. You do not need a legal team for the first draft. You need five things written down.

Scope. Name exactly what can be used and where. "My voice, for the intro of this one podcast, on these two platforms." A blank check reads as "anything, anywhere, forever," and that is almost never what you meant.

Term. Put an end date on it. Permission that never expires is permission you gave away. A clear term forces a renewal conversation, which is your chance to renegotiate or walk.

Kill switch. Write down how you revoke, and how quickly they have to take it down once you do. Control you cannot exercise is not control.

Pay. State whether it is a flat fee or per use, and when it is paid. "We will figure out money later" is how the money quietly becomes zero.

Byline. Say how you are credited, so your name follows the real thing and stays off anything fake made in your name.

This is the difference between a friendly nod and something you can actually stand on. A short, plain email that both people confirm in writing does the job for most creator deals.

The next time someone asks to use your likeness, do not answer out loud. Answer in writing, with these five.
