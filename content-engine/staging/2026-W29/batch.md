## Angle 1: Why one-line prompts give you mush

**Graphic:** IG `why-prompts-fail-1x1.png` (1080×1080) · LI/FB `why-prompts-fail-4x5.png` (1080×1350) · data `why-prompts-fail.json`

### Instagram Post

If AI keeps giving you bland, generic answers, the problem is not the AI. It is the one-line prompt.

You type "write me a caption" and hit enter. The AI has no idea who you are, who it is talking to, or what good even looks like. So it guesses. And a guess comes out as mush.

It is not being lazy. It is filling in five blanks you never gave it. Who should it be. What does it need to know. What is the actual task. What shape should it take. How do you know it worked.

Skip those and you get the average of the whole internet. Fill them in and you get something that sounds like you.

The short prompt feels faster. It just moves the work to the cleanup after.

Save this the next time an AI answer comes back flat.

### LinkedIn Post

Most people are trying to write shorter prompts. The real skill is writing deeper ones.

A one-line prompt feels efficient. "Draft a follow-up email." Done in three seconds. Then the answer comes back generic, you rewrite half of it, and the three seconds turn into fifteen minutes. The speed was an illusion.

Here is what actually happens when you type a bare prompt. The model has no role, so it picks a bland default voice. It has no context, so it writes for no one in particular. It has no clear task, so it hedges. It has no format, so the shape is random. And it has no idea what good looks like, so it hands you the safest, most average version of the answer.

That is five missing pieces, and the model quietly fills each one with a guess. The output is the sum of five guesses. No wonder it reads like it was written by a committee that has never met you.

The fix is not a longer prompt for its own sake. It is a prompt that answers the questions the model would otherwise guess at. Role. Context. Task. Format. Checks. When you supply those, the guessing stops and the quality jumps.

Working deeper here saves you the cleanup later. The minute you spend framing the ask is cheaper than the fifteen you spend fixing a lazy one.

## Angle 2: The five layers of a prompt that works

**Graphic:** IG `the-five-layers-1x1.png` (1080×1080) · LI/FB `the-five-layers-4x5.png` (1080×1350) · data `the-five-layers.json`

### Instagram Post

Every prompt that actually works has the same five layers. Miss one and the answer gets weaker.

Role. Tell the AI who to be. A booking agent writes differently than a copywriter.

Context. Give it what it needs to know. Your business, your audience, the situation.

Task. One clear ask. Not three things at once.

Format. The shape you want back. Short email. Bulleted list. Two options.

Checks. What good looks like. Sounds like me. No fluff. Under 100 words.

Stack those five and the AI stops guessing. It starts working from your world instead of the whole internet.

You do not need clever prompt tricks. You need these five layers, every time.

Save this and use it on your next prompt.

### LinkedIn Post

People keep asking me for the secret prompt. There is no secret prompt. There is a structure, and it has five layers.

Work deeper on the framing and you stop needing tricks. Here is the whole thing.

1. Role. Tell the model who it is. "You are a booking agent for a Latin band." A role sets the voice, the priorities, and the assumptions before you ask for anything.

2. Context. Give it the facts it cannot know. Who you are, who the audience is, what happened before this, what constraints apply. This is the layer most people skip, and it is the one that does the most work.

3. Task. State one clear ask. Not "help me with my emails." A single, specific job the model can actually finish and you can actually judge.

4. Format. Describe the shape of the output. A three-sentence reply. A table. Two options to choose from. If you do not name the shape, you get whatever the model defaults to.

5. Checks. Tell it what good looks like. "Sounds like me, no corporate tone, under a hundred words." Now the model has a target instead of a vibe.

That is it. Role, Context, Task, Format, Checks. The order matters less than the completeness. Every layer you fill in is one less thing the model has to guess.

This is what I mean by Human-Led AI. You are not writing longer prompts. You are doing the thinking the model cannot do for you.

Try all five on one prompt today and watch the quality change. Link in first comment.

### LinkedIn Post

The most useful hour I have spent with AI was not writing a clever prompt. It was rebuilding a bad one, layer by layer, until I trusted it.

For a while I used AI to help with the first pass on booking inquiries. My prompt was one line. "Write a reply to this lead." The results were technically fine and completely unusable. Too formal, too generic, zero warmth. I was rewriting every one from scratch, which meant the AI was saving me nothing.

Instead of giving up, I rebuilt the prompt with the five layers I teach.

Role. "You are my booking assistant for a Latin band in San Diego." Context. My typical rates, my availability, the kind of events I play, the tone I use with clients. Task. One job, draft a warm reply to this specific inquiry. Format. Three or four sentences, casual, no corporate stiffness. Checks. It should sound like me and never overpromise on a date I have not confirmed.

The difference was not subtle. The next draft came back sounding like me on a good day. I went from rewriting every reply to lightly editing one in ten.

That is the lesson I keep coming back to. The model was never the problem. My brief was. Working deeper on the prompt did more than any tool switch ever could.
