Most beginners are trying to get faster answers out of AI. The people who actually get value are working deeper, not faster. They fixed a handful of small habits first.

I watch new users hit the same five walls, over and over:

Blind trust. They paste the result straight into a client email without reading it. When it is wrong, and it sometimes is, their name is on it.

## Angle 2: The fix, context before command

**Graphic:** IG `the-context-first-fix-1x1.png` (1080×1080) · LI/FB `the-context-first-fix-4x5.png` (1080×1350) · data `the-context-first-fix.json`

### Instagram Post

The reason your AI answers feel generic is you are giving it a command with no context.

Most beginners jump straight to the ask. Write me a caption. Draft an email. The model has nothing to work with, so it hands you something bland that could belong to anyone.

Flip the order. Context first, command second. Give it four things before you ask for anything.

Role. Who is it being for you. Facts. The real details only you know. Goal. What a good result actually looks like. Format. The shape you want back.

Same tool, completely different output. You are not writing a better prompt. You are handing it a better brief.

Save this and load the context before you type the ask.

### LinkedIn Post

The biggest beginner mistake with AI is not a bad prompt. It is a rushed one. People type the command and skip the context, then blame the tool for a generic answer.

The fix is to work deeper before you work faster. Load context first, give the command second. I teach it as four things you provide before you ever ask for output:

Role. Tell it who it is for this task. Booking assistant, plain-spoken editor, patient tutor. This alone reshapes the whole answer.

Facts. Hand it the real details only you have. The venue, the date, the client, the constraints. A model cannot know your world unless you tell it, and the specifics are what make an answer yours instead of anyone's.

Goal. Say what a good result looks like. Warm not stiff. Under a hundred words. Something a busy venue owner would actually reply to. If you cannot name the target, the model cannot hit it.

Format. Describe the shape you want back. Three options, a short email, a bullet list. Save yourself the reformatting.

Do those four and the command at the end can be one plain sentence. The context did the heavy lifting.

This is what staying in the loop looks like in practice. You're not slowing it down. You're briefing it the way you'd brief a new hire.

## Angle 3: A real fix, turning a weak prompt strong

### Instagram Post

Watch a weak prompt turn strong in three moves.

Weak. Write a post about my music workshop. That is what most beginners type, and the answer comes back generic every time.

Add who. Who is it for and in what voice. Working musicians in San Diego, in my plain everyday tone.

Add why. What is the point. To get a few of them curious enough to ask a question.

Add facts. The real details only you have. The date, the room, what people actually walk away with.

Then edit it so it sounds like you, not the machine. The AI drafts. You are the one who has to stand behind it.

### LinkedIn Post

The most common thing I hear from beginners is my AI results are boring. Almost always the prompt is the problem, and the fix is to work deeper, not to hunt for a magic phrase.

Here is a real before and after I walk people through.

Before: "Write a post about my workshop." Nine words, no context. The model gives back something that could describe anyone's workshop, because you told it nothing that made yours yours.

Now we add the missing layers, one at a time.

Who. "For working musicians in San Diego, in a plain, peer to peer voice, no hype." The tone shifts immediately.

Why. "The goal is to get a few of them curious enough to reply with a question." Now the model has a target to aim at instead of just filling space.

Facts. "It is a Saturday morning session, hands on, and they leave with one workflow they can use Monday." Real details are what make copy specific and believable.

Then the human step, the one people skip: read it fully and edit it so it sounds like you. The model can draft. It cannot be you, and your audience can tell the difference.

Four moves, maybe two minutes. The output goes from forgettable to something you would actually put your name on. That last part is the whole point. The AI drafts, you stay in the loop, and the judgment stays yours.

Try this on one weak prompt today and watch what the added context does.
