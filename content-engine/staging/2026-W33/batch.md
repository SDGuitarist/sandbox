## Angle 2: Is it worth automating

### Instagram Post

Not every task is worth handing to AI. Here is the quick test I run before I automate anything.

Often. Do I actually do this every week? If it happens once a year, automating it is not worth the setup.

Rules. Does it follow the same steps every time? If it needs my judgment on every case, a tool will just make new messes.

Cheap miss. What happens if the AI gets it wrong? If a mistake is cheap and easy to catch, go for it. If a mistake is expensive, keep a human hand on it.

Three quick questions. Often, rules, cheap miss. If a task passes all three, it is a good candidate. If it fails one, do it yourself for now.

This keeps you from automating things that never needed it.

Save this before you build your next AI shortcut.

### LinkedIn Post

Most advice says automate everything you can. The more useful skill is knowing what not to automate at all.

Automation is not free. It costs setup time, and a bad automation runs its mistake over and over while you are not watching. So before I hand a task to AI, I run it through three checks.

1. Often. Do I repeat this on a regular basis? A weekly report is worth automating. A thing I do once a quarter usually is not. The time you save has to beat the time you spend building the shortcut, and rare tasks rarely clear that bar.

2. Rules. Does it follow the same steps each time, or does every case need a fresh decision? Tasks with clear rules are a good fit. Tasks that live on judgment are not, at least not without you in the loop, because the model will confidently apply the wrong rule.

3. Cheap miss. What does a mistake cost? If a wrong answer is low stakes and easy to spot, automate freely. If a wrong answer is expensive or hard to catch, keep a person checking the output.

A task that passes all three is worth building. A task that fails even one is better done by hand for now.

This is what staying human led looks like in a small business. Not automating everything. Automating the right things, on purpose.

Run your next automation idea through these three before you build it. Which check does it fail?

### LinkedIn Post

Most people try to roll out AI across their whole business at once. That is exactly why it stalls. The move that actually works is smaller than that.

When I started building AI into my own small business, I did not touch ten things. I touched one. Here is the pattern I would give anyone starting out.

Pick one. Look at your week and find the single task you dread most or lose the most time to. For me it was following up with venues, which I could never keep up with by hand.

Build small. Set up one system that does only that. Resist the urge to make it do five other things while you are in there. Narrow is what makes it work.

Use it. Run that one system for a full month without changing it. This is the step people skip. You learn what it actually does for you only by living with it, not by tweaking it.

Then add. Once that first system is solid and earning its place, and only then, let yourself build the next one.

This is slower than the big rollout, and that is the point. One system you trust beats ten you abandoned. The compounding comes from finishing, not from starting.

What is the one task you would put first? Start there, and give it a full month before you build anything else.
